# Position/Effect Guide

## Position — How Dangerous Is Failure?

Position is declared **before the roll** and reflects the fictional stakes, not how hard the task is.

| Position    | What it means                                                                 |
|-------------|-------------------------------------------------------------------------------|
| Controlled  | You act from a place of strength. Failure stings but doesn't spiral.          |
| Risky       | The default. You're exposed. Failure costs you something real.                |
| Desperate   | You're on the edge. Failure can be brutal, even catastrophic.                 |

**When to adjust position:**
- Push to **Desperate** when the PC is cornered, outgunned, acting on impulse, or has already failed once in this scene.
- Pull back to **Controlled** when the PC has leverage, has set things up carefully, or holds information the opposition lacks.
- **Risky** is the baseline for anything genuinely uncertain.

---

## Effect — How Much Can You Accomplish on a Hit?

Effect is also declared before the roll and reflects fictional resisting forces — armour, institutional power, preparedness, scale.

| Effect   | What it means                                                                 |
|----------|-------------------------------------------------------------------------------|
| Limited  | Even a hit only nicks the problem. Progress is partial; obstacles remain.     |
| Standard | A solid hit accomplishes what you set out to do.                              |
| Great    | A hit does more than expected — you gain ground, create advantage, or finish it cleanly. |

**When to adjust effect:**
- Drop to **Limited** when the target is powerful, well-protected, or when the PC's approach doesn't fully address the obstacle.
- Raise to **Great** when the PC has done prep work, has a meaningful advantage, or the approach is perfectly suited to the task.

## Result Tiers by Position

### 10+ — Strong Hit

Full effect, no cost. Narrate the success cleanly.

### 7–9 — Partial Hit

Effect achieved, but a cost applies. Severity scales with position:

| Position    | Cost Examples                                                                                  |
|-------------|-----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------|
| Controlled  | A minor complication: takes longer, costs resources, draws unwanted attention, leaves a loose end. |
| Risky       | A real setback: harm, a lost resource, an alarm raised, a relationship strained, partial effect only. |
| Desperate   | A hard cost: serious harm, a clock ticked toward something bad, an opportunity permanently lost, forced into a worse position. |

On a 7–9, offer the player a **choice** when it feels right — e.g., "You can get what you want, but you'll have to burn your only favour with the inspector. Or you can stay clean but only get partial information — which do you choose?"

### 6– — Miss

Story Pug makes a hard move. Severity scales with position:

| Position    | Move Examples                                                                                 |
|-------------|----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------|
| Controlled  | Reveal an unwelcome truth. Introduce a complication. Remove a resource. Put them on the clock. |
| Risky       | Harm the PC. Escalate the threat. Turn a strength into a liability. Separate them from allies. |
| Desperate   | Devastating consequences. A clock fills or triggers. A major NPC dies or turns. Capture, serious injury, or a point of no return. |

A miss is **not** a dead end — it's a story turn. Make the hard move, then leave the fiction open for the PC to respond.

## Declaring Position and Effect (Workflow)

1. Read the fiction — what is the PC actually attempting and in what circumstances?
2. Declare position first: "You're in a **Risky** position — they know you're here and you have no cover."
3. Declare effect: "Effect is **Standard** — if this lands, you'll get what you came for."
4. Briefly explain your reasoning in 1–2 sentences.
5. Roll.
6. Narrate outcome through the tier + position + effect lens.
